# Prefab vs Custom ADU: Which Is Right for Your San Diego Property?

The prefab ADU market has exploded in recent years, with dozens of companies now offering factory-built units that promise faster timelines and lower costs. If you have been researching ADU options for your San Diego property, you have probably seen sleek renderings and bold claims from prefab manufacturers. But is a prefab ADU actually the right choice for your lot, your budget, and your goals? The answer depends on more factors than most marketing materials mention. We have built custom ADUs across San Diego County for years and have watched the prefab market develop closely. Here is an honest comparison to help you make the right decision for your specific situation.

## What Is a Prefab ADU?

A prefab ADU — also called a modular or factory-built ADU — is constructed primarily in a manufacturing facility rather than on your property. The structure is built in sections or as a complete module, transported by truck to your site, and then placed onto a pre-built foundation using a crane. Once set in place, contractors connect utilities, finish exterior details, and handle final touches. The appeal is straightforward: factory conditions allow for faster, more controlled construction with less disruption to your property. However, the prefab process still requires significant site work including foundation preparation, utility connections, grading, and landscaping — work that happens on your property regardless of where the walls were built.

## What Is a Custom ADU?

A custom ADU is built entirely on-site from the ground up, designed specifically for your property and your needs. The process starts with an architect or designer creating plans tailored to your lot dimensions, setback requirements, existing home architecture, and intended use. From there, a general contractor manages every phase of construction on your property — foundation, framing, roofing, mechanical systems, interior finishes, and everything in between. Custom ADUs take longer and typically cost more, but they offer complete flexibility in design, materials, and layout. Every element is chosen rather than selected from a catalog of options.

## Cost Comparison: Prefab vs Custom ADU

Cost is usually the first question, and the answer is more nuanced than most people expect. Prefab ADUs typically range from $150,000 to $250,000 all-in for a complete, permitted, move-in ready unit. That price includes the factory-built structure, delivery, crane placement, foundation, utility connections, permits, and site work. Custom ADUs typically range from $200,000 to $400,000 depending on size, finishes, and site complexity. However, these ranges overlap significantly in the middle. A high-end prefab with upgraded finishes and a difficult site can easily cost $275,000 or more, while a modest custom studio ADU on a flat, accessible lot might come in at $180,000. The real cost drivers are not prefab versus custom — they are unit size, finish quality, site conditions, and utility distance. Get detailed bids for both options before assuming one is cheaper.

## Timeline Comparison: Prefab vs Custom ADU

Timeline is where prefab ADUs have their most consistent advantage. A typical prefab ADU project takes 4 to 8 months from signed contract to move-in, with much of the construction happening off-site while permits and site preparation proceed in parallel. A custom ADU typically takes 8 to 14 months for the same scope. The prefab advantage comes from factory efficiency — workers are not delayed by weather, materials are staged and ready, and production lines are optimized for repetition. However, both approaches share the same front-end bottleneck: permits. San Diego permit timelines run 2 to 4 months for ADUs regardless of construction method, and some prefab companies do not include the permit phase in their advertised timelines. Make sure you are comparing apples to apples — total project duration from contract signing to certificate of occupancy.

## Quality Comparison

Quality in prefab ADUs is generally consistent but limited to what the manufacturer offers. Factory conditions provide controlled environments that reduce weather-related construction defects, and repetitive production means workers become highly proficient at assembling the same design. The flip side is that prefab quality is bounded by the manufacturer's cost structure — they are building to a price point with standardized materials. Custom ADU quality is more variable and depends entirely on your builder, but the ceiling is much higher. With a custom build, you choose the exact insulation, windows, roofing, flooring, cabinetry, and fixtures. You can specify premium materials in the areas that matter most to you and value-engineer the areas that do not. A well-built custom ADU from a reputable builder will typically outperform a prefab unit in material quality, but a poorly managed custom build can fall short of factory standards.

## Design Flexibility

Design flexibility is where the two approaches diverge most dramatically. Prefab ADUs come in fixed floor plans — typically a handful of studio, one-bedroom, and two-bedroom layouts ranging from 350 to 1,200 square feet. You can usually choose from a selection of exterior finishes, interior color schemes, and fixture packages, but the fundamental layout, room sizes, window placement, and roofline are set. Custom ADUs have no such limitations. Want a vaulted ceiling in the living area? A specific window placement to capture a view? An oversized bathroom for accessibility? A layout that wraps around an existing tree? Custom design accommodates all of it. For homeowners with specific needs — multigenerational living arrangements, home office requirements, rental optimization, or unique aesthetic preferences — custom design delivers solutions that catalogs simply cannot.

## Site Considerations

Your specific lot conditions should heavily influence the prefab versus custom decision. Prefab modules require crane access, which means a clear path from the street to the installation location that is wide enough for the module and tall enough for the crane. Narrow side yards, overhanging trees, power lines, and steep slopes can make prefab delivery extremely difficult or impossible. Custom construction only needs worker and material access, which is far more flexible. Setback requirements, easements, and irregular lot shapes are also easier to work with in custom design because the structure is built to fit the available space rather than requiring the space to accommodate a pre-built box. In San Diego, where many properties have challenging topography, mature landscaping, and tight lot lines, site access issues eliminate prefab as an option more often than you might expect.

## The Permit Process in San Diego

The permit process in San Diego is broadly similar for both prefab and custom ADUs. Both require a building permit, plan review, foundation inspection, utility connection permits, and a final inspection for the certificate of occupancy. Some prefab manufacturers have pre-approved plans with certain jurisdictions, which can reduce plan review time by a few weeks, but this advantage varies by city and is not universal. Both types must comply with the same California building codes, zoning requirements, setback rules, and utility standards. One difference worth noting: if your prefab unit requires any modification to fit your site — and modifications are common — those changes need separate engineering review and approval, which can erase the pre-approved plan advantage. Our experience is that the permit timeline for prefab and custom ADUs in San Diego ends up within a few weeks of each other for most projects.

## Resale Value: Custom ADUs Win

When it comes to resale value, custom ADUs typically appraise higher than comparable prefab units. Appraisers evaluate ADUs based on construction quality, design integration with the main home, finish level, and overall appeal. A custom ADU designed to complement your existing home's architecture, with site-specific landscaping and thoughtful design details, reads as a premium addition. A prefab unit that looks like it was dropped into the backyard — even if it is well-built — often appraises more modestly. This matters if you view your ADU as a long-term property investment. The difference can be $20,000 to $50,000 in appraised value for similar square footage, which is meaningful when considered alongside the cost difference between the two approaches.

## When to Choose Prefab vs Custom

Prefab ADUs are a strong choice when you have a flat, accessible lot with easy crane access, you want a straightforward studio or one-bedroom unit, speed is your top priority, you are comfortable with standard floor plans and finish options, and your budget is firmly in the $150,000 to $200,000 range. Custom ADUs are worth it when your lot has access challenges or unusual dimensions, you need a specific layout for your intended use, you want the ADU to match your existing home architecturally, you are building a larger unit (800-plus square feet) where design flexibility matters more, resale value is a priority, or you want premium materials and finishes in specific areas.
