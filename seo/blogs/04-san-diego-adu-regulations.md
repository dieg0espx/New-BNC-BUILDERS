# San Diego ADU Regulations: Everything Homeowners Need to Know (2026)

California has the most ADU-friendly laws in the country, and San Diego homeowners are in a particularly strong position to take advantage of them. Over the past several years, the state legislature has passed a series of bills that have systematically removed the barriers that once made building an accessory dwelling unit difficult, expensive, or outright impossible. If you have been researching San Diego ADU regulations, you may have found conflicting information because the rules have changed so frequently. This guide reflects the current regulatory landscape as of 2026, covering everything from lot requirements and setbacks to the permit process and timeline.

Several landmark California laws form the foundation of current ADU regulations in San Diego. SB 9, signed in 2021, allows lot splits and duplexes on single-family zoned properties, effectively increasing density without rezoning. AB 68 and AB 881 streamlined the ADU approval process by requiring cities to approve compliant applications within 60 days and prohibiting many of the restrictions local jurisdictions previously imposed. Most recently, AB 1033, which took effect in 2024, allows homeowners to sell their ADU as a separate unit through a condominium-style arrangement, creating an entirely new pathway for affordable homeownership. Together, these laws mean that virtually any single-family property in San Diego County has the potential to add an ADU, and local jurisdictions have limited ability to impose additional restrictions beyond what state law allows.

One of the most common questions homeowners ask about San Diego ADU regulations is whether their lot qualifies. The answer, in the vast majority of cases, is yes. California law prohibits minimum lot size requirements for ADUs, meaning even smaller lots can accommodate a unit as long as setback and coverage requirements are met. For Junior ADUs (JADUs), which are units of 500 square feet or less created within the existing footprint of the primary residence, there are essentially no lot size restrictions whatsoever. Most single-family residential lots in San Diego, Escondido, San Marcos, Vista, and surrounding cities can support at least one ADU and one JADU, giving homeowners the potential to add two units to their property.

Setback requirements are among the most important San Diego ADU regulations to understand because they determine where on your lot the unit can be built. For new detached ADUs, state law establishes a maximum setback requirement of 4 feet from the rear and side property lines. This is significantly less than the standard setbacks for primary residences, which are often 10 to 20 feet. For garage conversions and other ADUs created within existing structures, no setbacks are required beyond what the existing structure already provides, even if the existing structure is built to the property line. Attached ADUs that share a wall with the primary residence follow the setback rules of the primary structure for shared walls and the 4-foot rule for new walls. Front setbacks are generally governed by the underlying zoning and vary by jurisdiction.

Height limits for ADUs in San Diego have been significantly relaxed under recent state legislation. Detached ADUs are generally permitted up to 16 feet in height, which comfortably accommodates a single-story unit with standard ceiling heights. Two-story and attached ADUs can reach 25 feet in some zones, particularly when built above garages or when the ADU is attached to a multi-story primary residence. Some specific zones may allow additional height, and projects within half a mile of major transit stops may qualify for further height allowances under state law. It is important to check the specific zoning for your property, as height limits can vary between the City of San Diego, Escondido, and other incorporated cities within the county. Our team verifies the exact allowable height for every project during the feasibility phase.

Parking requirements for ADUs in San Diego have been largely eliminated, which is one of the most impactful regulatory changes for homeowners. State law prohibits parking requirements for ADUs located within half a mile of a public transit stop, which covers a significant portion of developed San Diego. Parking requirements are also eliminated for ADUs that are converted from existing garages, carports, or accessory structures regardless of transit proximity. Even where parking requirements technically apply, the maximum a jurisdiction can require is one space per unit, which can be tandem or covered. In practice, the vast majority of ADU projects in San Diego County proceed with no additional parking requirement, and converting your existing garage into an ADU does not trigger a replacement parking requirement for the spaces you are losing.

Owner-occupancy rules have been another area of significant change in San Diego ADU regulations. Prior to recent state legislation, many jurisdictions required the property owner to live on-site, either in the primary residence or the ADU. As of 2025, owner-occupancy requirements have been largely eliminated for standard ADUs. This means you can build an ADU and rent both the primary residence and the ADU to tenants, which is a significant benefit for investors and homeowners who may relocate. JADUs still require owner-occupancy of either the primary residence or the JADU itself. Additionally, properties with ADUs approved under older regulations that included owner-occupancy deed restrictions may still be subject to those requirements.

The ADU permit process in San Diego follows a fairly predictable sequence, though timelines vary by jurisdiction. The process begins with a pre-application consultation, where you or your contractor meets with the planning department to confirm zoning, setbacks, and any site-specific requirements. Next comes plan preparation, including architectural drawings, structural engineering, Title 24 energy calculations, and any required surveys or reports. The plan check phase, where the jurisdiction reviews your submittal for code compliance, takes 4 to 8 weeks by law, though the City of San Diego and Escondido often complete reviews within 30 to 45 days. Once plans are approved, you receive your building permit and construction can begin. During construction, the building department conducts inspections at key milestones including foundation, framing, rough mechanical and plumbing, insulation, and final inspection.

From start to finish, homeowners should plan for a total ADU timeline of 9 to 14 months. The pre-construction phase, including design, engineering, and permitting, typically takes 3 to 6 months. Construction itself runs 4 to 8 months depending on the complexity of the project, weather conditions, and any inspection delays. Garage conversions and JADUs tend to sit at the shorter end of both ranges, while large detached units with custom features take longer. Material procurement and lead times can impact the schedule as well, so we recommend finalizing selections for major items like windows, cabinets, and appliances early in the design phase to avoid construction delays.

While the broad strokes of ADU regulations are set by state law, there are meaningful differences between jurisdictions in San Diego County that affect your project. The City of San Diego and the City of Escondido, for example, have different fee structures, different plan check timelines, and different interpretations of certain state provisions. Impact fees, which fund infrastructure improvements, vary by jurisdiction and can add $5,000 to $20,000 to your project depending on the ADU size and location. Some jurisdictions offer fee waivers or reductions for ADUs under certain square footage thresholds. Escondido, where our office is based, has been proactive about streamlining the ADU process, and we have strong working relationships with the planning and building departments throughout the county.

The most common mistakes homeowners make when navigating San Diego ADU regulations fall into a few categories. Starting construction without permits is the most serious error and can result in stop-work orders, fines, and requirements to demolish unpermitted work. Underestimating the timeline leads to frustration and poor decision-making when permits take longer than expected. Choosing a contractor who is unfamiliar with ADU-specific regulations results in plans that get rejected, adding weeks or months to the timeline. Failing to account for utility connection requirements, particularly sewer and water, can create budget surprises mid-project. And overlooking HOA restrictions, which cannot prohibit ADUs but can impose certain design standards, sometimes leads to conflicts that delay construction.
